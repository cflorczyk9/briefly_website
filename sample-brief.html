Breadth was uniformly ugly. Defensive sectors like utilities and staples underperformed as the market rotated into energy. Tech weakness was selective—semis under pressure but AI infrastructure plays holding relatively firm. This is a market pricing in oil-driven inflation risk and delayed Fed cuts, punishing multiple-expansion trades while rewarding energy upside.

For the week, the benchmark Treasury yield jumped about 14bp to 4.16%—the largest weekly gain since April—as inflation fears from oil rallies outweighed dovish signals from jobs weakness, with traders now expecting just one Fed rate cut this year instead of two. This reflects a critical regime shift: bond yields are rising despite job losses because energy shock inflation is dominating the narrative. In the current March 2026 climate, bonds are falling alongside stocks, leaving gold as one of the few functional diversifiers left. Real yields remain pressured as inflation expectations become unanchored by Middle East supply fears.
